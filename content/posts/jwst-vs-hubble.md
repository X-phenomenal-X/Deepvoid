The most common misconception about the James Webb Space Telescope is that it's "Hubble, but sharper." It's more accurate to say Webb sees a different universe.

## Why infrared matters

The universe is expanding, which stretches light travelling across it. Light that left the first galaxies as visible light arrives at Earth stretched into infrared — wavelengths Hubble largely can't see. To look at the earliest galaxies, you don't need a bigger visible-light telescope. You need an infrared one.

Infrared also passes through dust. Star-forming regions that look like opaque smoke to Hubble are transparent nurseries to Webb — which is why Webb's images of the Pillars of Creation show thousands of newborn stars Hubble simply couldn't detect.

## The engineering price

Seeing in infrared means seeing heat — so the telescope itself must be colder than its targets. Webb operates at about -233°C behind a tennis-court-sized sunshield, parked 1.5 million km from Earth at a gravitational balance point called L2. Hubble orbits 540 km up and has been repaired by astronauts five times. Webb is too far away to ever service. It had over 300 single-point failures during deployment. All of them worked.

## What it found

In its first years Webb has spotted galaxies that formed earlier than models comfortably predicted, measured the atmospheric chemistry of planets around other stars, and imaged carbon dioxide on worlds light-years away. The telescope designed to see the first light in the universe is also our best tool for asking whether anyone else is out there.

Hubble is still working, by the way. The two telescopes observe together — one in visible light, one in infrared — giving us a stereo view of the cosmos no single instrument could.

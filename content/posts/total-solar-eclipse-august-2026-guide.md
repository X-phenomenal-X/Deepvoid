On the evening of August 12, 2026, the Moon's shadow will race across the North Atlantic at over 2,000 km/h, and for the first time since 1999, totality will touch mainland Europe. If you have ever wanted to see a total solar eclipse — the only astronomical event that routinely makes adults gasp out loud — this is the most reachable one for billions of people this decade.

We built a [live countdown and full guide](/eclipse) that ticks down to the second. Here's the longer story.

## The numbers that matter

The eclipse begins when the Moon's umbra first touches Earth in the Arctic Ocean at **17:02 UTC**. Greatest eclipse — the deepest, longest totality — comes at **17:45 UTC** near the Faroe Islands, where the Sun disappears for **2 minutes 18 seconds**. The shadow then sweeps southeast, crossing northern Spain around sunset, and leaves Earth at **18:30 UTC**. Ninety minutes, three landmasses, one shadow.

## Where to stand

**Iceland** gets the headline act. This is Iceland's first total solar eclipse since 1954 and its *only* one this century — the next doesn't come until 2196. Reykjavík sits inside the path and gets about 1 minute 30 seconds of totality, with the Sun comfortably above the western horizon. Every hotel room on the island will be booked. Book now or know someone with a couch.

**Spain** is the accessible option. Totality crosses the north of the country — its first since 1905 — in the early evening. Depending on where you stand, totality lasts from about 30 seconds near the path edges to 1 minute 44 seconds on the center line. One catch: the Sun will be low. Find a spot with a clean, open view to the west — a hilltop, a coastline, a wide plain. A mountain on your western horizon will cost you the whole show.

**Greenland** is for the committed. The umbra crosses the island mid-path under some of the statistically clearest skies on the route. If you're the type who plans expeditions, this is yours.

**Everyone else** still gets something: a partial eclipse will be visible across most of North America, western Europe, and west Africa. Even at half coverage, the light turns thin and silvery and shadows go strange. Step outside.

## The double feature nobody is talking about

The Perseids — the most beloved meteor shower of the year, up to 60 meteors an hour — peak on the *same night*, August 12–13, under a nearly moonless sky. Watch the Sun vanish at sunset, stay out as the sky darkens, and catch shooting stars until dawn. There may not be a better single night of skywatching this decade. ## The one rule that is not negotiable

Never look at the partial phases without certified eclipse glasses rated **ISO 12312-2**. Sunglasses don't work. Stacked sunglasses don't work. Your phone camera pointed at the Sun doesn't work (and may not survive). Only during the brief minutes of *totality* — when the Sun's disc is completely covered and the corona appears — is it safe to look with your naked eye. Glasses sell out everywhere in the final weeks before every eclipse; buy them months early.

## Why totality is worth a trip

A 99% partial eclipse is interesting. A 100% total eclipse is a different event entirely. In the final seconds, daylight collapses, the temperature drops, birds go quiet, and the Sun's corona — a million-degree atmosphere invisible on every other day of your life — suddenly hangs in a dark sky like a white flower. Veterans of the 2017 and 2024 American eclipses will tell you: the difference between 99% and 100% is the difference between standing outside a concert hall and being inside it.
